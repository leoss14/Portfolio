Investigating Gender Price Differences in UK Retail Products

Classifying products by target gender using text and color features, then testing for price differentials across 12,800 supermarket products.

Products 12,832
Accuracy (vs Human) 84.4%
Features 799
Training Samples 2,532

Overview

This project examines whether products marketed to women are priced higher than comparable products for men in UK supermarkets — the so-called "pink tax." It proceeds in two stages: first, building a classifier to identify gendered products from text and image features; then, running a series of regressions to estimate the price gap while controlling for product category, store, and product characteristics.

The classifier achieves 80% F1 using TF-IDF and color features. The regression analysis finds that female products are ~9% cheaper in raw comparisons, but the gap becomes statistically insignificant after controlling for product category (p = 0.35). The unit price regression shows essentially zero difference (+0.7%, p = 0.92). The main finding is that apparent price differences reflect category composition — women's and men's products fall into different product types — rather than within-category pricing discrimination.

Background

The Problem

Research on the "pink tax" examines whether products marketed to women are priced higher than equivalent products marketed to men. Estimating this requires labeling products by target gender, but many products don't state this explicitly. Instead, they signal gender through color choices, language, and brand names.

With ~21,000 products in this dataset, manual labeling is impractical. This project attempts automated classification, though with caveats about accuracy and the inherent subjectivity of "gendered marketing."

Approach

The classifier combines three feature types:

Text features: TF-IDF vectors from product descriptions (500 features) and category breadcrumbs (200 features). These capture explicit gender terms ("for men"), brand names (Venus, Gillette), and product categories.

Color features: Dominant colors extracted from product images using K-means clustering, mapped to 31 named colors. This produces 93 binary features (3 color slots × 31 colors).

Metadata: Store ID, price, and unit price (6 features).

Data

Sources and Filtering

The dataset contains 21,436 products scraped from Tesco, Sainsbury's, Boots, and Superdrug. I excluded categories unlikely to be gendered (food, electronics, pet supplies, cleaning products), leaving 12,832 products in categories like personal care, cosmetics, and toiletries.

For training labels, I used two sources: (1) explicit gender terms extracted via regex from product text ("for women", "men's", etc.), yielding 1,075 female and 844 male products; and (2) 259 products with human-coded labels from a separate validation exercise.

The training set was balanced to 844 samples per class (female, male, neutral), with neutral samples drawn from products lacking explicit gender terms.

Color Extraction

For 2,000 products, I downloaded images and extracted the three most dominant colors using K-means clustering on pixel RGB values. Each color was then matched to the nearest named color from a 31-color palette.

This process succeeded for 1,133 products; the rest failed due to missing images or download errors. The chart below shows sample products with their extracted colors, including some errors where background elements skewed the results.

Validation Against Human Labels

The test set above uses labels derived from explicit text patterns, which may not reflect how humans would categorize products. A stricter test uses 256 products with independent human labels.

Agreement with human coders across different feature sets. The combined model reaches 84.4%.

The combined model (breadcrumbs + descriptions + colors) achieves 84.4% accuracy against human labels. Breadcrumbs alone reach 77.3%, performing well on neutral products (99%) but poorly on female products (57%). Description-only performs worst at 60.8%.

The confusion matrices show the model is conservative—it tends to predict "neutral" when uncertain, leading to under-classification of gendered products.

Estimating the Pink Tax

Prior Research

The "pink tax" refers to the observation that consumer products marketed toward women are often priced higher than comparable products aimed at men. Research on the topic has produced mixed findings. Gonzalez Guittar et al. (2022) studied over 3,000 products and found some evidence of a premium on specific items such as deodorants and lotions, though men paid more for shaving products. Moshary et al. (2023), using Nielsen scanner data with product ingredient controls, concluded that price gaps largely reflect differences in product composition rather than discriminatory pricing.

Both studies agree that gender segmentation in consumer products is widespread, but diverge on whether the resulting price differences constitute a "tax" or simply reflect product differentiation. This analysis contributes to the question using UK grocery data, testing whether a female price premium persists after progressively controlling for product category, store, and product characteristics.

Approach

With the classification pipeline providing gender labels, the next step is to estimate the price differential between female- and male-targeted products. The analysis uses log-transformed prices as the dependent variable, which means coefficients can be interpreted as approximate percentage differences. All regressions use heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors (HC1).

The key question is whether the female coefficient remains significant as controls are added. If the gap disappears after controlling for product category, it suggests composition effects (women's products fall in different price ranges) rather than within-category pricing discrimination.

Raw Price Comparison

Among the 1,919 explicitly gendered products (1,075 female, 844 male), female products have a lower mean price (£7.03 vs £8.15) and lower median (£4.00 vs £4.50). This is the opposite of what the pink tax narrative predicts.

The R² jump from 0.006 to 0.672 when adding category fixed effects is the key result. Product category explains the vast majority of price variation. The raw negative gap arises because female-targeted products are concentrated in lower-priced categories (personal care, period products) while male-targeted products span a wider price range (including grooming sets and premium razors). Within the same category, there is no statistically detectable difference.

This aligns with Moshary et al.'s conclusion that price differences between gendered products are largely explained by product differentiation rather than discriminatory pricing.

Variance explained by each specification. Category controls account for the largest share of price variation.

Gendered vs Ungendered Products

A three-way comparison using all 12,832 products (with ungendered products as the reference group) shows that both gendered categories are cheaper than ungendered products, but female products especially so. Female products are 16.4% cheaper than ungendered ones (p < 0.001), while male products are 2.9% cheaper (not significant). This suggests gendered products as a whole occupy lower price tiers than the broader product mix.

Price distributions across all three gender groups. Ungendered products span a wider and higher price range.

Quantile Regression

OLS estimates the effect on the conditional mean. Quantile regression tests whether the gap differs across the price distribution. Without category controls, the gap is significantly negative at the 10th, 50th, and 90th percentiles (female products are 12–20% cheaper), but zero at the 25th and 75th percentiles. With category controls, the pattern reverses at the bottom of the distribution: at the 10th and 25th percentiles, female products are 25–43% more expensive within the same category (p < 0.001), while the gap is zero at higher quantiles.

The controlled quantile results suggest that the cheapest female products within a category tend to be priced higher than the cheapest male equivalents. This could reflect a floor-price effect — budget female products start at a higher baseline, even if median and premium products are priced similarly. However, the small number of categories and products within each means these estimates should be treated cautiously.

Variation by Store

Running the raw-gap regression separately by store reveals substantial heterogeneity. One retailer (Morrisons) shows a large and significant female discount of 18.6% (p < 0.001), while the other two stores show no significant gap. A female × store interaction model confirms this: the interaction for Morrisons is significant (p < 0.001) with a total female effect of −50.6%, while the other stores show no significant interaction.

Left: female price premium by store (thick border = p < 0.05). Right: mean prices by gender and store.

Conclusions

On Classification

The classifier reaches 80% F1 (gradient boosting) on the test set and 72% agreement with independent human labels. Text features (category breadcrumbs and description TF-IDF) carry most of the predictive signal, with color features adding a modest +2.3 percentage points. The model is conservative — it under-classifies gendered products rather than over-classifying neutral ones.

On Pricing

The regression analysis finds no evidence of a systematic pink tax at the aggregate level. Female products are 9–10% cheaper in raw comparisons, and the gap becomes statistically insignificant (and reverses sign to +3.7%) after controlling for product category. The unit price regression shows no difference at all (+0.7%, p = 0.92).

Within categories, results are mixed: greeting cards show a small female premium, shower products show a male premium, and most categories have too few gendered products for meaningful comparison. The quantile regression suggests a possible floor-price effect for the cheapest products within a category, but the evidence is limited by sample size.

These findings are consistent with the prior literature. As in Moshary et al. (2023), the apparent price gap is largely a composition effect — female and male products fall into different categories with different price levels — rather than within-category discrimination. The meaningful finding is the extent of gender segmentation itself, not the prices.

Limitations

Gender labeling: Products are classified using explicit keyword matching and a trained classifier. Both approaches miss implicitly gendered products — items marketed toward a gender through color, imagery, or brand positioning without using explicit terms. The human-coded sample (259 products) showed that keyword-based labels miss roughly 23% of gendered products.

Category granularity: Only 11 product categories had sufficient products of both genders for within-category comparison. Finer-grained categories would improve comparability but reduce sample sizes further.

Product equivalence: The regression controls for category and description text, but does not account for product quality, ingredients, or size beyond what unit price captures. A "women's razor" and a "men's razor" in the same breadcrumb category may differ in blade count, handle design, or packaging, which could justify price differences.

Color data coverage: Color features are available only for Morrisons products (~5,600 of 12,800). Tesco and ASDA image URLs had expired at the time of extraction. Color-based findings should be interpreted with this coverage limitation in mind.

Retailer scope: The data covers three UK grocery retailers. Results may not generalise to other product categories (clothing, electronics) or other markets.
